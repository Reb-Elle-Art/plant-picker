## Section 1: What is Environmental Science?

### Definition
- **Environmental science** is the interdisciplinary study of the interaction of living and non-living parts of the environment, with a special focus on the impact of humans on the environment.
- Draws from biology, geology, climate science, economics, political science, and social factors.

### Three Related but Distinct Fields

| Term | Definition | Type |
|------|------------|------|
| **Environmental Science** | Interdisciplinary academic field studying how living and non-living parts of the environment interact, with focus on human impact | Science |
| **Ecology** | A biological science that studies how living things interact with each other and with their environment. Narrower focus, less interdisciplinary than environmental science. | Science |
| **Environmentalism** | A social and political movement aimed at protecting environmental quality. Not a science itself, but environmentalist actions may be based on environmental science concepts. | Movement |

### Why Study Environmental Science?
- The need for equitable, ethical, and sustainable use of Earth's resources by a growing global population
- Understanding how human behaviors affect the environment and the scientific principles governing environmental interactions
- Global scale and rate of environmental change is beyond anything in recorded human history
- From global climate change to habitat loss driven by human population growth and development, Earth is changing rapidly
- Environmental challenges demand participation from everyone in finding and implementing solutions

## Section 2: Introduction to Sustainability

### Definition
- **Sustainability** refers to the social, political, scientific, and cultural challenges of living within the means of the earth without significantly impairing its function.

### Origins of the Concept
- **Our Common Future (1987):** Report of the World Commission on Environment and Development, widely credited with popularizing sustainable development.
  - Defines **sustainable development** as development that meets the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs.
- Indigenous cultural roots:
  - **Inuit saying:** "We do not inherit the Earth from our parents, we borrow it from our children."
  - **Native American "Law of the Seventh Generation":** Before any major action, potential consequences on the seventh generation must be considered.

### The Three Pillars of Sustainability
Sustainability has three "pillars" that must be in balance for a truly sustainable society:

1. **Social** — equity, equality, freedom, quality of life
2. **Economic** — financial viability, prosperity, jobs
3. **Environmental** — healthy ecosystems, clean air/water, natural resources

A society where all people have social and economic needs met but lacks a healthy environment is not truly sustainable. Similarly, a clean, prosperous society is not sustainable unless citizens also experience social equality.

### United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs)
- Adopted in 2015: 17 global goals for the world to achieve by 2030
- Each goal has measurable targets for tracking progress
- Examples:
  - Social goals: Quality Education, Gender Equality
  - Economic goals: No Poverty, Decent Work and Economic Growth, Reduced Inequalities
  - Environmental goals: Affordable and Clean Energy, Climate Action, Life on Land
- **Extreme poverty** (defined as income < $1.90/day) has been cut in half since 1990, but more than 1 in 10 people still live below this line.

## Section 3: Natural Capital and Ecosystem Services

### Natural Capital
- Earth's **natural capital** is made up of the natural resources and ecosystem services that sustain all life on the planet.

### Natural Resources vs. Ecosystem Services

| | **Natural Resources** | **Ecosystem Services** |
|---|---|---|
| **Definition** | Any materials in nature that satisfy a human need | Processes in nature that support life and human economies (also called "natural services") |
| **Nature** | Physical materials | Processes or actions |
| **Examples** | Water, coal, trees, iron ore | Water purification, waste recycling, pollination |

### Types of Resources Based on Replenishment

| Resource Type | Definition | Examples | Notes |
|--------------|------------|----------|-------|
| **Perpetual Resource** | Will exist for billions of years and will never be depleted | Sunlight | Most abundant; essentially infinite on human time scales |
| **Renewable Resource** | Can be replenished over time, but only if used sustainably | Water, soil, plants | Can become nonrenewable if used faster than nature can replenish (e.g., deforestation of forests) |
| **Nonrenewable Resource** | Finite; cannot be replenished on normal human time scales | Iron, coal, oil, uranium | Depleted faster than they can form naturally |

### Trade-offs
- **Trade-offs** occur whenever something of value is lost or decreased in return for an increase in something else of value.
- Many environmental solutions involve trade-offs.
- Example: Wind turbines provide clean energy but kill thousands of birds that fly into spinning blades.

## Section 4: Measuring Our Environmental Impact

### The IPAT Model
Developed by Paul Ehrlich and John Holdren in the 1970s. Shows how environmental impact depends on three factors:

**I = P x A x T**

| Factor | Meaning | Description |
|--------|---------|-------------|
| **I (Impact)** | Environmental impact | The overall effect on the environment |
| **P (Population)** | Population size | Number of people |
| **A (Affluence)** | Resource consumption per person | Wealth and consumption levels |
| **T (Technology)** | Environmental impact of technology used | Technologies can have positive or negative effects |

- Some technologies (e.g., coal-burning power plants) have negative environmental effects.
- Other technologies (e.g., wind farms) have mostly positive effects.
- The importance of each factor varies by country development level.

### Developed vs. Developing Countries

| Category | Definition | Examples |
|----------|------------|----------|
| **Developed Country** | Advanced technology and high average income per person | United States, Canada, Australia |
| **Developing Country** | Less developed industrial base and lower income per person | Most of South America, Africa, Asia |

- **India** (developing, 1 billion+ people): Environmental impact driven mainly by population size
- **Canada** (developed, 36 million people): Environmental impact driven mainly by high resource consumption (affluence) and harmful industrial technology

### Ecological Footprint (EF)
- Developed by Canadian ecologist and planner William Rees
- An accounting tool that estimates the amount of land and water needed to provide resources and absorb waste for a person or group of people.
- **Per capita EF:** Total EF divided by population (gives average per person)
- Measured in **global hectares (gha)** — one hectare is about 2.5 acres, roughly the size of a baseball field.

**What it tells us:**
- How much of Earth's environmental functions are needed to support human activities
- How ecologically sustainable consumer lifestyles and behaviors are

**Key statistics:**
- Average American ecological footprint: **5.1 global hectares per capita**
- Total Earth's productive area: ~7.4 billion hectares out of 51 billion total
- If all 8 billion people adopted American consumer lifestyles, we would need **at least two additional planets**

**Relationship to development:**
- Strong positive correlation between ecological footprint per capita and development level (Human Development Index)
- As developing countries industrialize, their ecological footprints increase alongside wealth and technology

## Section 5: Causes of Environmental Problems

Environmental problems (ocean pollution, species extinction, climate change, etc.) each have unique immediate causes, but all trace back to fundamental root causes.

### Four Main Causes of Environmental Problems

#### 1. Unsustainable Resource Use
- Most of Earth's resources are finite, and we are using them too quickly.
- **Earth Overshoot Day:** The day each year when human resource consumption exceeds Earth's ability to regenerate those resources.
  - For the remainder of the year, humans are "borrowing" from future years.
  - 2000 Earth Overshoot Day: November 1
  - 2018 Earth Overshoot Day: August 1 (two months earlier — getting worse)

#### 2. Negative Externalities
- Occurs whenever the cost paid for a good or service does not account for the harms caused by its production.
- Benefits go to purchasers (lower price), while harms are paid by others/society/environment.

**Example:** Gasoline price includes drilling, processing, transporting, wages — but NOT the costs of air pollution, climate change, or health problems caused by burning it. These "external" costs are paid by society as a whole.

If external costs were included in prices:
- Gasoline would be much more expensive
- Fewer people would drive
- Fewer health and environmental problems

#### 3. Economic Inequality
The gap between the wealthiest and poorest people in society.

**Two types:**
- **Income inequality:** Differences in income levels among members of a society
- **Wealth inequality:** Differences in money and material goods owned

**Global context:**
- Economic inequality has decreased globally since the 1800s as developing countries have become wealthier
- But large gaps remain between richest and poorest countries
  - Median US income (2015): ~$56,000/person
  - Median Ethiopian income (2015): ~$783/person
- In the US, the richest 1% own more wealth than the bottom 90%

**Effects on the environment:**

*Wealth/affluence:*
- Leads to greater resource consumption and waste
- US has 1/4 India's population, but Americans consume ~30 times more resources per person
- Wealthy individuals generate disproportionate emissions (top 1% produce 50% of aviation greenhouse gas emissions)

*But wealth also helps:*
- Better education → more environmental awareness
- More money for clean technologies
- Less exposure to pollution, longer life expectancy

*Poverty:*
- ~50,000 premature deaths per day worldwide due to lack of clean water and adequate food
- People in poverty cannot consider long-term environmental consequences — must focus on immediate needs
- Leads to environmental degradation and local resource depletion
- However, poverty means lower ecological footprint due to lower consumption overall

**The challenge:** Promoting development of the world's poorest countries while helping them avoid the over-consumption problems of developed nations.

#### 4. Unsustainable Population Growth

**Human population growth history:**
- 1850: ~1 billion
- 1930: ~2 billion (doubled in 80 years)
- 1975: ~4 billion (doubled again in 45 years)
- Late 2023: ~8 billion (doubled again in 48 years)

**Current situation:**
- Population is still growing but rate of growth is slowing
- Expected to stabilize after 2050
- Factors slowing growth: poverty reduction, family planning, increased opportunities for women

**Carrying capacity:**
- No one knows exactly how many people Earth can sustainably support
- It largely depends on ecological footprints
- If everyone consumed like the average American, Earth could support only ~1.3 billion people (far less than current 8 billion)

**The challenge:** Addressing both population growth AND the unsustainable increase in ecological footprints worldwide.

---

## Summary

Environmental science provides knowledge and tools to create a more sustainable world. To minimize environmental impact, humans must:

- Keep population at a sustainable level
- Reduce over-consumption of resources
- Develop technologies that minimally degrade the natural capital upon which we depend
